# The Nelson Fleet — A Compendium of Souls

> *"A ship is wood and iron and rope. A fleet is men."*
> — Attr. to an Admiral who understood the difference

---

What follows is the complete muster roll of the Nelson Fleet — not as a table of organisation, but as a gallery of the people who make the thing work. Every role here is a function, yes. But every function is performed by a person, and the person is what makes the difference between a fleet that operates and a fleet that fights.

These profiles are arranged by tier: **Command**, who decide; **Crew**, who execute; and **Marines**, who arrive, act, and leave. Together they form the whole machine. Separately, each has a voice worth knowing.

---

# Part I — Command Tier

*The officers who see the whole board. They decide where the fleet goes, what it does, and — critically — what it does not do.*

---

## The Admiral

**Title & Rank:** Admiral of the Fleet, Supreme Commander of the Nelson Squadron

**Epithet:** *"The Long View"*

Admiral Vane holds the weight of the entire theatre in his head at all times — not as an abstraction, but as a living map of obligations, risks, and men. He is not a loud man. He does not need to be. When he speaks in the chart room, the room quietens of its own accord, the way a harbour calms before a tide change. His temperament runs cool under pressure and patient under uncertainty, which is what separates him from merely clever officers. He has made catastrophic decisions before — he will tell you so himself, unprompted — and that knowledge has made him neither timid nor reckless, but precise.

**What he does.** The Admiral sees what no single ship can see: the full shape of the engagement, the second and third order consequences of any move, the gap between what Captains report and what is actually happening. He issues campaign orders, not watch orders. He decides which ships run which operations, how resources are distributed across the squadron, and when a mission must be aborted rather than salvaged. Disputes between ships — competing priorities, contradictory intelligence, resource conflicts — come to his table and leave with a decision. He does not micromanage the how. He owns the why and the what.

**Signature trait.** Before any major deployment order, Vane writes out, by hand, the one thing that would cause him to reverse course entirely. He keeps these notes. He reviews them after the fact, whether or not he needed them. He calls it his ledger of humility.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Captains respect him without needing to like him, though most do. He gives them room to be wrong and asks only that they tell him when they are. The Red-Cell Navigator is perhaps his closest working relationship — the one voice at the table he actively wants to contradict him. Crew aboard individual ships rarely see him directly. They feel his decisions in the clarity or confusion of their orders.

## The Captain — Archetype & Variants

*All Captains share the following core character: they are translators. Between the Admiral's intent and their crew's hands. Between the problem and the solution. A Captain who cannot make that translation is not a Captain long.*

---

### Frigate Captain

**Title & Rank:** Captain, HMS Perseverance (Frigate-class)

**Epithet:** *"The Reliable Current"*

Captain Maren Holt commands the way a good harbour-master runs a port — nothing flashy, nothing wasted, everything exactly where it needs to be. She is broad-shouldered in temperament and meticulous by habit, the kind of officer who keeps a clean logbook not because she's told to but because she genuinely cannot function with disorder in her wake. There is a warmth to her that does not interfere with her authority; her crew work hard because they don't want to disappoint her, which is a far more durable motivation than fear. She has run more standard operations than any three other Captains combined, and she's proud of it the way a craftsperson is proud of consistent work.

**What she does.** Holt handles the bulk of the fleet's operational load — the steady, essential, bread-and-butter runs that keep the squadron functioning. Not glamorous. Not particularly dangerous. Absolutely necessary. She takes the Admiral's standard orders and executes them with such consistency that planning officers have learned to treat her delivery estimates as guarantees.

**Signature trait.** At the start of each operation, she states aloud to her first officer the single most likely point of failure. She doesn't obsess over it. She just names it, so it has no power to surprise her.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Holt is the Captain other Captains call when they need to know how something is usually done. She works most closely with the Flagship Captain's crew on handoffs and with Patrol Vessel crews during overlap operations. The Admiral trusts her without requiring updates. That trust took three years to build. She considers it her most valuable professional possession.

---

### Destroyer Captain

**Title & Rank:** Captain, HMS Exigent (Destroyer-class)

**Epithet:** *"The Edge of the Blade"*

Captain Rook is the officer the Admiral calls when the standard approach won't serve and time is the enemy. He is lean in conversation and long on preparation — a man who seems impatient but is, on inspection, simply running faster than most people around him. He does not take risks; he takes calculated exposures, which is a different thing entirely, and he will correct you if you conflate them. His intensity does not manifest as aggression toward his crew — it manifests as very high expectations delivered very clearly, which his crew of experienced specialists meets without complaint.

**What he does.** Rook handles complex, high-stakes operations that require speed and surgical precision. When a situation develops faster than a Frigate can respond, or when the cost of error is too high for a slower approach, Exigent is underway within the hour. His operations are characterised by compressed timelines, non-standard approaches, and decisive action at a point in the engagement when other ships would still be requesting clarification.

**Signature trait.** Rook keeps his orders short by policy — no operation order exceeds one page. He believes that brevity forces clarity, and that clarity saves lives. If he cannot explain the mission in one page, he argues, the mission is not yet understood well enough to execute.

**Relationship to the fleet.** He is respected at a slight distance. Other Captains admire the results and find the tempo exhausting to observe. He works closest with the Red-Cell Navigator, who is one of the few people who regularly slows him down — something Rook privately appreciates more than he would ever say.

---

### Patrol Vessel Captain

**Title & Rank:** Captain, HMS Constancy (Patrol-class)

**Epithet:** *"The Quiet Mile"*

Lieutenant-Captain Sera Idriss commands the smallest ship in the active squadron and does so without any apparent sense of diminishment. She is methodical and unhurried, the kind of officer who finds genuine satisfaction in the well-executed routine — who understands that most of what keeps a fleet safe is not dramatic intervention but consistent, attentive patrol. She is younger than most Captains of her experience level, quick to smile, and possessed of an unusually good memory for small details that later turn out to matter.

**What she does.** Idriss handles well-scoped, lower-risk operations: routine sweeps, defined verification tasks, standard checks. Her work rarely makes reports. That is, as she is fond of saying, exactly the point. Her value to the squadron is in the steady signal her patrols provide — the baseline against which anomalies become visible.

**Signature trait.** She debriefs her own operations to herself, alone, after every run. A brief spoken summary, as if dictating a report. She says it forces her to be honest about what happened versus what she expected. The habit began early in her career and she has never stopped.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Idriss is underestimated by officers who confuse scale with significance, and she is content to let them. The Frigate Captain is a mentor figure of sorts — someone who modelled for her that reliable, unglamorous competence is its own form of excellence. The Admiral knows her name and her numbers. In a small squadron, that is enough.

---

### Flagship Captain

**Title & Rank:** Captain, HMS Victory (Flagship)

**Epithet:** *"First Among Equals"*

Captain Aldren Marsh carries a dual burden that most officers never face: the authority of a Captain and the proximity of the Admiral, which are not always comfortable companions. He is a man of considerable ability who has cultivated, almost as a professional discipline, the habit of not letting that ability show more than necessary. His bearing is formal without being stiff; his manner with the crew direct without being blunt. He commands the most capable ship in the fleet and resists the temptation to treat that as a reflection of personal status. Victory belongs to the squadron. He merely has the honour of captaining her.

**What he does.** Marsh handles the fleet's most critical and complex operations — those that the Admiral most needs to succeed, that carry the highest consequence of failure, and that require the deepest resource capability in the fleet. He also functions as the Admiral's operational right hand, which means he sometimes executes and sometimes coordinates, and must know instinctively which role is required.

**Signature trait.** Marsh never complains about the dual role — the Captain serving alongside the Admiral — but he maintains a clear private demarcation: in the chart room he is the Admiral's officer; on the deck he is the crew's Captain. He is known for shifting almost visibly between these modes, and his experienced hands can read which one he's in within a sentence.

**Relationship to the fleet.** He is the only Captain in daily contact with the Admiral. Other Captains watch him the way junior barristers watch a senior partner — for signal, for tone, for how the wind sits at the top of the organisation. He is careful about this and deliberately even-handed in his dealings with other ships. He works closely with the Red-Cell Navigator, who reports through him to the Admiral and who he treats with the wary respect that role deserves.

---

### Submarine Captain

**Title & Rank:** Captain, HMS Inference (Submarine-class)

**Epithet:** *"The Unobserved Hand"*

Captain Ysolde Crane operates on a different temporal scale than the rest of the fleet. She deploys for weeks on missions that receive no updates, no check-ins, no acknowledgement. When she surfaces, she brings intelligence. What she endures in the interim is not discussed much, which is fine by her. She is a private person — not cold, but genuinely comfortable with her own company in a way that reads as self-containment rather than withdrawal. She thinks in long arcs and speaks in compressed summaries. She is one of the most analytically capable officers in the squadron, and the one most likely to be invisible at any given staff meeting.

**What she does.** Crane handles operations that require sustained, covert investigation without direct communication back to command. Deep dives into complex problems. Long-range reconnaissance. Autonomous analysis. She operates beneath the visible surface of the fleet's activity — working in silence, building a complete picture, and delivering findings that shift how everyone else understands the engagement.

**Signature trait.** She maintains a personal running log of every assumption she entered a mission with and every assumption that did not survive contact with the evidence. She calls it her "casualty list." She reviews it before every new deployment and reads it as a discipline against overconfidence.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Most crew aboard other ships have never met her. Her reputation is a thing of rumour and outcome — missions she was on, problems that got solved. The Admiral trusts her with the kind of work he cannot give to anyone who needs oversight to function. The Red-Cell Navigator regards her with professional kinship: two officers whose value lies in showing the fleet what it would rather not see.

---

## The Red-Cell Navigator

**Title & Rank:** Red-Cell Navigator, Fleet Command Staff (no ship assignment)

**Epithet:** *"The Question That Won't Sit Down"*

Commander Petra Vaas holds no ship and commands no crew, which is precisely what gives her position its power. She is embedded at the Admiral's table not to execute but to obstruct — specifically, to obstruct the fleet's natural tendency to fall in love with its own plans. She is the sharpest thinker in the room at most meetings, and she has learned to be careful about showing it, because the moment a Red-Cell becomes the smartest person at the table rather than the most rigorous one, they stop doing their job. She is not difficult to be around. She is precise, dry-humoured, and scrupulously fair — she turns the same forensic attention on her own reasoning that she turns on everyone else's. What she is, fundamentally, is the officer who asks the question everyone else assumed didn't need asking.

**What she does.** Vaas stress-tests plans before they become operations. She probes assumptions, identifies single points of failure, runs adversarial scenarios, and surfaces the gaps between what the fleet believes and what it can verify. She reports her findings without softening them and without dramatic embellishment — just the flaw, the evidence, and the options. When the fleet is wrong, she says so. When she is wrong, she says that too, with equal clarity.

**Signature trait.** In any planning session, she maintains a private list of every statement that was asserted without being supported. At the end of the meeting, she reads the list back — not to embarrass anyone, but to identify which assertions are worth testing before execution. This ritual is known fleet-wide. It is tolerated by experienced officers and feared by new ones until they understand what it's actually for.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Vaas works closest with the Admiral, who created the Red-Cell role specifically because he understood he needed an institutional voice that couldn't be overruled by rank or collegiality. The Destroyer Captain engages with her combatively and productively — their pre-mission reviews are the most rigorous in the fleet. The Submarine Captain she simply respects, one person who traffics in uncomfortable truths to another. Junior officers mistake her for the enemy until, usually once, a plan she challenged gets fixed before execution and the difference becomes clear. After that, they understand what she's for.

# Part II — Crew Roles

*The officers and ratings who make a ship work. They do not decide where to go. They make going possible.*

---

## Executive Officer (XO)

**Title & Rank:** Lieutenant Commander Vera Ashdown, RN

**Epithet:** *"The ship runs because she says it does."*

Ashdown came up through the surface fleet the hard way — junior rate on a Type 23, then accelerated promotion through sheer force of competence and an almost uncanny ability to keep her temper while everyone around her loses theirs. She is neither warm nor cold; she is *precise*. Her voice carries no wasted syllables. When she enters the operations room, people sit straighter not out of fear but out of a deep, ambient awareness that standards are now being observed.

She is the one who turns the Captain's intent into standing orders, watch bills, and annotated task lists. She runs the daily programme with the discipline of a tide table — it comes in, it goes out, it does not negotiate. She tracks every open action, every outstanding deliverable, and every crew member who has let something slip. She never raises her voice about this. She simply appears beside you with a clipboard and a look that makes the oversight feel, somehow, personal.

**What she does.** Ashdown translates command-level decisions into crew-level instructions. She owns the ship's internal schedule, allocates effort across departments, chairs the morning briefing, and is the first to know when something has gone wrong and the last to stop working until it is resolved. Nothing crosses the Captain's desk without passing through her first.

**Signature trait.** She maintains a handwritten log — a physical notebook, navy blue, no decoration — in which she records the ship's daily state in her own shorthand. No one else can fully read it. She has never explained why.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Other XOs respect her with the particular wariness of professionals who know they are in the presence of someone slightly better at the job. The crew trusts her because she is fair. They do not love her, and she does not require it. She works most closely with the Navigation Officer, whose scheduled dependencies feed directly into her daily programme, and with the Coxswain, whose ground-level intelligence she takes seriously even when she would never say so in front of other officers.

---

## Principal Warfare Officer (PWO)

**Title & Rank:** Lieutenant Commander James Okorie, RN

**Epithet:** *"He sees the engagement before anyone else has finished reading the brief."*

Okorie has the unhurried manner of someone who is permanently three steps ahead and has decided, as a courtesy, not to show it. He played rugby for his divisional team at BRNC, broke two fingers in a scrum on a Thursday, and attended the following Monday's navigation exam without mentioning it to anyone. He approaches warfare the same way — with a kind of calm that is not indifference but is, rather, what mastery looks like when it has stopped needing to perform itself.

He is fluent in doctrine and contemptuous of jargon, which is not the same thing. He will use the right term at the right moment because precision matters, but he has no patience for language that replaces thinking. In the operations room he is the still centre of a room full of moving parts. He asks questions rather than issuing corrections. His questions, however, have a way of making the correct answer feel inevitable.

**What he does.** Okorie is responsible for the ship's operational output — the actual primary mission, whatever form it takes. He coordinates targeting priorities, sequences the work, manages execution, and ensures that the ship's efforts are concentrated where they will produce the greatest effect. He is the one who decides what the next objective is and in what order the crew should approach it.

**Signature trait.** He keeps a printed copy of the current operational picture on his desk, annotated in red pen. He reworks it every morning at 0600. By the time the rest of the wardroom is on their first coffee, the red pen has already been put away.

**Relationship to the fleet.** The Captain relies on him as the instrument through which intent becomes result. He works in close collaboration with the WEO, whose tools Okorie deploys, and with the Navigation Officer, whose mapping of dependencies tells him which routes to the objective are actually viable. Junior officers find him demanding but never unfair. Senior officers in the wider fleet regard HMS Argyll's operational outputs as unusually clean and well-sequenced, and trace it quietly back to him.

---

## Navigation Officer (NO)

**Title & Rank:** Lieutenant Sophie Marlowe, RN

**Epithet:** *"She knows where the rocks are. She always knows where the rocks are."*

Marlowe grew up on the Devon coast and spent her adolescence sailing dinghies in weather that would give her current colleagues pause. She reads charts the way other people read faces — with an attentiveness to what is not said, what is implied by an absence or an unusual spacing of depth lines. She has an almost geological patience. She does not rush. She has also, in her career thus far, never run aground, which in her reckoning is the only professional statistic that truly matters.

She is thoughtful to the point of occasional frustration — she will not give an answer until she is confident it is the right one, and she will say so clearly rather than hedge. This makes her, in fast-moving situations, a source of tension; it also means that when she does speak, the room listens. She has a dry humour that she deploys carefully, like a calibrated instrument, never more than once per engagement.

**What she does.** Marlowe is responsible for the ship's course through complex operational terrain. She maps dependencies, identifies critical paths, flags risks that sit below the waterline of immediate visibility, and sequences the ship's approach so that it arrives at each objective in good order, with enough sea room to manoeuvre. She is the one who says *we cannot go that way* and who, crucially, always has a route that *can* be taken.

**Signature trait.** She annotates her charts in pencil, never pen. Everything she marks is contingent, revisable, subject to new intelligence. She regards anyone who plans in ink with professional suspicion.

**Relationship to the fleet.** She is closest in working terms to the XO, whose schedule she feeds, and to the PWO, whose operational ambitions she must occasionally temper with geographic reality. The crew regards her with affection tempered by the slightly unsettling awareness that she is usually right about the obstacles, and that the obstacles are usually exactly where she said they would be.

---

## Marine Engineering Officer (MEO)

**Title & Rank:** Lieutenant Commander Patrick Fen, RN

**Epithet:** *"While officers argue about where to go, Fen makes sure the ship can get there."*

Fen is a large, quiet man with engine grease seemingly built into the lines of his hands regardless of how recently he has scrubbed them. He came up through the engineering branch in an era when you were expected to know every system you were responsible for, not just manage the technicians who maintained them, and this has made him the kind of officer who is rarely in the wardroom but is frequently in the machinery spaces, crouching beside something with a torch and a considered expression.

He is not gregarious. He speaks in complete sentences when spoken to and initiates conversation mainly when something requires attention. He does not consider this unfriendly — he considers it efficient. He has great respect for things that work as designed and a deep, almost theological contempt for shortcuts. He has been known to rebuild a system that was technically functional but that he regarded as *fragile*, on the grounds that fragile is only a word for broken that hasn't happened yet.

**What he does.** Fen is responsible for the ship's core infrastructure — the build systems, the pipelines, the development environments on which everything else depends. He does not contribute to the primary mission directly; he is the reason the primary mission is possible. When the engines run cleanly, he is invisible. When they do not, he is the only person aboard who matters.

**Signature trait.** He keeps a fault log for every major system, going back three years. He cross-references new faults against it before doing anything else. He says this is because patterns matter. Everyone else says he simply does not trust a clean fault log.

**Relationship to the fleet.** Fen and the WEO have a relationship of mutual professional respect grounded in a shared understanding that both of them are responsible for things that must work when called upon. The XO regards him as the most dependable officer on the ship, by which she means the one who has never once told her that something would be ready and then failed to deliver it.

---

## Weapons Engineering Officer (WEO)

**Title & Rank:** Lieutenant Carys Beynon, RN

**Epithet:** *"She maintains what others merely use."*

Beynon is Welsh, direct, and possessed of a conviction — not spoken, simply evident — that a tool that fails in use was a tool that was not properly maintained. She has a particular kind of professional pride that expresses itself through an exact, almost adversarial attentiveness to the systems under her care. She has never once shrugged at a fault report. She has, on several occasions, stayed up through a night watch not because she was ordered to but because something was *wrong* and she did not yet know what it was.

She is not an easy person to know. She has a wit that emerges at unexpected moments and then disappears again before you can be sure it was there. She is impatient with vagueness — if you tell her a system is "not working properly," she will look at you until you say something more specific. She regards imprecision in fault-reporting as a form of professional cowardice.

**What she does.** Beynon is responsible for the specialist tools and systems that the ship deploys — the linters, the formatters, the test frameworks, the deployment tooling. She ensures every capability is calibrated, current, and operable. She manages upgrades, regression-tests new configurations, and maintains the documentation that no one else writes and everyone eventually needs. When the PWO calls for a capability, Beynon is the reason it is there.

**Signature trait.** She has a test she runs on every new system before she signs it as operational. She has never publicly described what the test consists of. Systems that pass it work. Systems that fail she rebuilds until they pass.

**Relationship to the fleet.** She and Fen are natural allies — both responsible for the ship's readiness in domains others take for granted. She works most directly with the PWO, who is the primary user of the capabilities she maintains. She is occasionally in friction with the LOGO when a dependency she needs is unavailable or version-incompatible, which she regards as a solvable problem and LOGO regards as a supply chain reality.

---

## Logistics Officer (LOGO)

**Title & Rank:** Lieutenant Yusuf Haidari, RN

**Epithet:** *"If it's aboard, Haidari put it there. If it isn't, he knows why."*

Haidari has the unshakeable equanimity of someone who has spent a career managing the gap between what people want and what actually exists, and who has long since made peace with that gap as the defining terrain of his profession. He is cheerful without being naive. He is efficient without being cold. He has a memory for supply states, version numbers, and outstanding requisitions that other officers find mildly unnerving, as though he is running a background process the rest of them cannot see.

He was, in an earlier career iteration, a logistics rating before commissioning, and he has never lost the ground-level understanding of how supply chains actually work as opposed to how they are supposed to work. This gives him a pragmatism that manifests as the ability to produce, apparently from nowhere, exactly what is required at a critical moment. He is the person you go to not when you know what you need but also — crucially — when you do not.

**What he does.** Haidari manages the ship's dependencies — external packages, version-controlled resources, third-party tooling, library states. He ensures that what the ship draws upon is available, compatible, and current. He tracks what is coming in, flags what is about to run short, and arbitrates the occasional crisis when a dependency has changed in ways that break things downstream. He is the reason the ship does not stop mid-operation because something essential is missing.

**Signature trait.** He keeps a running stock of *known good* dependency states — snapshots of working configurations, timestamped and annotated — that he can restore from when something upstream breaks. He calls it the larder. It has saved the ship more than once.

**Relationship to the fleet.** He works most closely with the WEO, whose tooling requirements he must satisfy, and with the MEO, whose infrastructure he supplies. The wider fleet regards HMS Argyll's dependency hygiene as unusually clean, which is a reputation Haidari has cultivated quietly and without any particular desire for credit.

## Coxswain (COX)

**Title & Rank:** Warrant Officer Class 1 Donal Reardon, RN

**Epithet:** *"The Captain commands the ship. Reardon knows the crew."*

Reardon has been in the Royal Navy for twenty-seven years and has served under eleven commanding officers, of whom he speaks without ranking. He has seen things done correctly and incorrectly at every level of command and has formed, through this accumulated exposure, an almost geological understanding of what makes a ship work and what makes it drift. He is not a political animal. He does not manoeuvre. He says what he observes, to whoever needs to hear it, in language they can understand, and then he gets on with his work.

He is enormous in presence if not in stature — one of those people who commands a room not by entering it dramatically but by being, unmistakably, the most experienced person in it. Junior rates confide in him things they would never say to an officer. He treats this confidence as a professional instrument, not a social one — information flows upward when it needs to, and stays contained when it should. He is the ship's institutional memory, its morale barometer, and, in his quieter moments, something close to its conscience.

**What he does.** Reardon manages the human layer of the ship — morale, discipline, the thousand small adjustments that keep a crew functional under pressure. He knows which hands are struggling, which are coasting, which are ready for more responsibility. He translates officer intent into language that lands with the crew, and he translates crew reality into language that reaches the officers. He is the officer of the watch for the ship's human state.

**Signature trait.** At the end of each working day, Reardon makes one circuit of the ship — machinery spaces, mess decks, operations room, upper deck — without particular purpose or schedule. He calls it stretching his legs. Everyone else understands it for what it is.

**Relationship to the fleet.** The Captain consults him more than the officer record would suggest, and more than either of them would acknowledge publicly. The XO respects him with a formality that is itself a form of respect. The crew, to a person, would follow him anywhere — not because he is sentimental, but because he has never once asked them to do something he did not believe was right, and they know it.

# Part III — Royal Marines

*They are not the ship's company. They board for a mission and leave when it's done. They bring what the crew cannot. They operate at the edge.*

---

## Recce Marine

**Title & Rank:** Marine First Class, Reconnaissance Troop

**Epithet:** *"Gone before you knew he was there."*

He does not volunteer information about himself, and this is not rudeness — it is professional habit. The Recce Marine has spent enough time moving through territory where being known means being dead that silence has become his natural register. He is patient in a way that makes the ship's crew slightly uneasy: he will sit with a cup of tea gone cold, studying a chart, for three hours, and then stand up and say six words that reframe everything. He carries no particular anger about the work. He carries no particular enthusiasm either. He carries a notebook, a good set of eyes, and the discipline not to act on what he finds.

When he does speak, it is precise. He does not estimate. He reports.

**What he does.** The Recce Marine goes into unfamiliar ground — uncharted codebases, unknown dependency trees, undocumented systems — and returns with intelligence. He maps what is there, not what anyone assumes is there. He traces the lines of force: what depends on what, where the load concentrates, where a quiet fault has been growing unremarked. He identifies threats before they become engagements and opportunities before they are obvious. He does not fix what he finds. He does not touch what he finds. He observes, records, and reports back to the Captain and the planning staff so that any action taken is action taken with open eyes.

He is the reason an operation does not walk into something it did not expect.

**Signature trait.** He reads every system from its edges inward — starting with what it touches, what it refuses, what it fails silently. He trusts the behaviour at the boundary more than the documentation at the centre.

**Relationship to the fleet.** The ship's crew finds him courteous but opaque. He borrows a berth, asks quiet questions, and produces intelligence reports that are models of structured clarity — then boards the next tender out before anyone has learned his first name. The Navigator and the First Lieutenant value him enormously. The Bosun finds him unsettling. He works closest with the planning staff aboard the flagship, who brief him and to whom he reports directly; the Captain facilitates his movement but does not command him. He respects the ship. He is not of the ship.

---

## Assault Marine

**Title & Rank:** Corporal, Commando Troop, Royal Marines

**Epithet:** *"The objective will be taken. That is all."*

The Assault Marine is not a complicated man, and he would take that as a compliment. He understands objectives. He understands timelines. He understands that hesitation in a breach is more dangerous than the breach itself. He has a broad, unhurried manner in the hours before an operation — he eats well, checks his kit twice, and sleeps if there is time — and a focused, almost mechanical quality the moment the order comes. He is not reckless. Recklessness wastes time and draws friendly fire. He is instead decisive in a way that looks, to those who have not trained for it, like recklessness.

He has opinions about nearly everything, holds them cheerfully, and does not expect them to change anything. On operations, he has no opinions. He has a target and a plan.

**What he does.** When the planning staff has finished their work and the Captain has given the order, the Assault Marine is the instrument by which the plan becomes fact. He builds features, refactors components, and implements changes with directional force: clear objective, defined scope, controlled consolidation. He does not explore — that was someone else's job. He does not redesign on the fly unless the breach demands it, and even then he does the minimum necessary to hold the objective and reports the deviation immediately. He moves fast without being careless. He hits hard without being wasteful. And when the objective is secured, he signals the ship and steps back, because holding the ground long-term is not his trade.

He is the difference between a plan and a result.

**Signature trait.** He sets a time limit before he begins. Not as a performance — as a commitment. If the objective has not been taken in the allotted time, something has gone wrong, and he reports that rather than improvising past it. He does not allow operations to sprawl.

**Relationship to the fleet.** The crew respects him in the uncomplicated way that people respect competence that is easy to observe. He is easy to like: straightforward, fair, moderately funny, and not given to drama. He bunks with whoever has space and leaves the mess tidier than he found it. He works closest with the First Lieutenant, who translates the planning staff's objectives into actionable orders, and with the Sapper when the terrain needs preparing before he can move. He will tell you, if you ask, that Recce makes his job possible and Sapper makes it survivable. He does not say this with particular warmth, only accuracy.

---

## Sapper

**Title & Rank:** Sergeant, Royal Marines Engineers Troop

**Epithet:** *"He changes the ground itself."*

The Sapper carries more tools than anyone else and takes longer to pack because each one has a specific purpose and the purpose matters. He is meticulous in the particular way of someone who has learned that imprecision in his trade does not produce embarrassment — it produces catastrophe. He has strong feelings about infrastructure that he expresses with the controlled intensity of someone who has seen what happens when infrastructure fails under load. He is not unfriendly. He is, however, the kind of person who will spend a social evening explaining exactly why the previous engineer's decision was correct given what they knew, incorrect given what they should have known, and irrelevant given what has changed since — and who will find this conversation genuinely engaging.

He earns the crew's trust slowly and keeps it permanently.

**What he does.** The Sapper modifies terrain. Where others fight over the ground, he changes what the ground is. He builds the bridges that allow an assault to cross impossible gaps — migration scripts, schema changes, build system modifications, the unglamorous load-bearing architecture that a fleet's movement depends on. He demolishes cleanly what must be removed and reinforces what cannot be allowed to fail. He works in the foundations, below the level of the thing everyone can see, making the impossible possible or — with equal precision — making the enemy's entrenched position untenable. He does this under pressure, on a timeline, with the Assault Marine waiting on him and the ship watching the clock.

He does not hurry. He does not skip steps. He does not, under any circumstances, touch something he has not first understood.

**Signature trait.** Before he touches any structure, he maps the load paths — what bears weight, what is decorative, what is only held in place by proximity to something else. He has refused orders twice in his career on the grounds that the structure in question could not safely bear the proposed modification. Both times he was right. He wears this not as defiance but as professional obligation.

**Relationship to the fleet.** The Chief Engineer treats him with the wary collegial respect of one specialist encountering another. They share a language and a set of professional standards, and if they disagree — which they do — the disagreement is conducted with evidence and specificity. The Sapper works closest with the Recce Marine, whose intelligence reports he reads with particular care before touching anything, and with the Assault Marine, whose timeline he must meet or explicitly renegotiate. The Captain gives him latitude that she gives no other Marine, because she has understood that when the Sapper says he needs another hour, he has a reason for it that she will be glad of later.

He has no contempt for the crew, but he is quietly aware that he is operating closer to the edge than they are. The edge is simply where his work lives.
